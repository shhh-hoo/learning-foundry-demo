# Why balanced-equation coefficients become mole ratios

A chemical equation first describes a ratio of reacting particles. In

```text
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
```

the particle ratio between Mg and MgO is `2:2`, which simplifies to `1:1`.

A mole is a fixed count of particles. Replacing each single particle with the same number of particles—one mole—does not change the ratio. Therefore the particle ratio and the mole ratio are numerically identical.

Balancing and mole conversion play different roles:

- balancing ensures that atoms are conserved;
- the coefficients state the relative particle counts;
- the definition of the mole lets the same ratio be used for amounts in moles.

## Common errors

1. Using `2:1` for Mg:MgO because the oxygen coefficient is 1.
2. Dividing only one side of `2:2`.
3. Treating subscripts inside a formula as reaction coefficients.
4. Saying “mass ratio” when the equation only gives a mole ratio.

## Diagnostic question

Before calculating, ask:

> Which two substances am I converting between, and what are their two coefficients?
